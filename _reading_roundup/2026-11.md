I spent a decent amount of time digging into the Coruna exploit kit - referenced last week. The NadSec RE and the Risky Business deep-dive are worth the time to read and digest.

Elsewhere... a breakdown in OPSEC by FancyBear, reverse engineering malware in the age of AI, and a physical pentest case study.

And, because process is my valentine, a non-technical pice about how bureaucratic process can eat organizational judgement.

**[Inside Coruna: Reverse Engineering a Nation-State iOS Exploit Kit From JavaScript](https://www.nadsec.online/blog/coruna)**
NadSec tore apart the 28 obfuscated JavaScript modules recovered from the Coruna watering-hole delivery infrastructure. Three independent WebKit RCE paths, a PAC bypass via confused-deputy, a JIT cage escape — and all 13 original payloads still live on the delivery domain as of this month.

The technical detals are great to dig through. But, the proliferation arc is worth sitting with too: allegedly built for a U.S. government client, exfiltrated by an insider, sold to Russian brokers, deployed against Ukraine, and eventually repurposed by Chinese actors for crypto theft.

**[A Ridiculously Deep Dive Into the Coruna Exploits](https://risky.biz/RBFEATURES5/)**
77-minute solo episode from Risky Business featuring James Wilson — former Apple software engineer, now enterprise tech editor — walking the full attack chain from watering-hole to full device compromise. His Apple internals background earns the "ridiculously deep" billing. Worth the runtime if the NadSec write-up left you wanting the audio commentary.

**[FancyBear Exposed: Major OPSEC Blunder Inside Russian Espionage Ops](https://ctrlaltintel.com/threat%20research/FancyBear/)**
A second exposed open-directory on an APT28/GRU C2 server — active for over 500 days — containing 11,000+ exfiltrated emails, 240+ stolen credential sets with 2FA secrets, and 140+ Sieve forwarding rules silently redirecting victim inboxes. Targets span Ukrainian prosecutors, Romanian and Greek military, Serbian defense, Bulgarian government. The lesson isn't new but it's worth repeating: *"sophisticated does not mean infallible."*

**[Malware Reverse Engineering is no longer a human problem!](https://x.com/fr0gger_/status/2028014798546378938)**
Roccia opened a conference keynote by silently submitting an unknown sample to his multi-agent pipeline, then gave his talk. Thirty minutes later he returned to the demo — static analysis, RE, OSINT enrichment, IOC extraction, YARA generation, ATT&CK mapping, full report, done. The write-up traces how he built to that point over four years. His conclusion: the skill he spent 15 years developing is becoming obsolete, and the new work is in orchestration and securing the pipelines themselves.

**[Access Log 001: The Rooftop](https://covertinstruments.com/blogs/access-logs/access-log-001-the-rooftop)**
A physical red team case study on gaining full access to a federal facility transitioning to a SCIF — no dramatic single exploit, just a chain of neglected small ones: forgotten rooftop access points, default panel credentials, passive badge cloning during public lunch hours, disabled alarms, legacy ESXi servers nobody was watching. OSINT from public building blueprints did the initial legwork. *"Real compromise rarely happens because of one dramatic mistake. It happens because enough small ones line up in the right order."*

**[Why 'Optimal' Solutions Fail in Complex Organizations](https://othjournal.com/2026/03/03/why-optimal-solutions-fail-in-complex-organizations/)**
Over The Horizon Journal essay on how large organizations — the Pentagon is the explicit frame — gradually replace judgment with process until the process becomes the point. The procedures that started as support mechanisms become the decision-makers, and what gets optimized is defensibility, not effectiveness. *"The result is an organization exceptionally good at producing decisions that survive scrutiny, but far less effective at producing decisions that survive change."* We aren't all the Pentagon, but a reminder worth heeding.
